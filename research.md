## Overview

This document presents the scientific research literature that underpins SkillsSync — an AI-powered platform that uses Natural Language Processing (NLP) and Large Language Models (LLMs) to extract, classify, and map competencies from student academic documents against real-time labor market requirements. The research spans five interconnected domains: the graduate employability crisis in Sub-Saharan Africa, NLP methods for skill extraction, AI applications in educational technology, optical character recognition for handwritten input, and job market competency frameworks.

## 1. The Skills Gap and Graduate Employability Crisis in Sub-Saharan Africa

The foundational problem that SkillsSync addresses — the systemic mismatch between university graduate competencies and employer requirements in Sub-Saharan Africa — is extensively documented in peer-reviewed literature and authoritative institutional reports. The following studies establish the empirical and policy basis for SkillsSync's intervention.

---

### 1.1 Quantifying the Mismatch: Africa-Wide Evidence

**Morsy, H., & Mukasa, A. (2020). 'Mind the mismatch?' Incidence, drivers, and persistence of African youths' skill and educational mismatches.**  
*African Development Review*, 32(S1).  
DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1111/1467-8268.12478](https://doi.org/10.1111/1467-8268.12478)  
Working Paper (AfDB WPS No. 326): [https://www.afdb.org/sites/default/files/documents/publications/wps_no_326_youth_jobs_skill_and_educational_mismatches_in_africa_f1.pdf](https://www.afdb.org/sites/default/files/documents/publications/wps_no_326_youth_jobs_skill_and_educational_mismatches_in_africa_f1.pdf)

This African Development Bank flagship study is the most authoritative quantitative analysis of educational and skill mismatches among African youth. Using panel labor market data, the authors find that **17.5% of employed African youth are overskilled, 28.9% are underskilled, 8.3% are overeducated, and 56.9% are undereducated**. Critically, the study demonstrates that mismatch is persistent over time — it does not self-correct through labor market adjustment alone. This structural rigidity is precisely what SkillsSync's AI-driven visibility and roadmapping tool is designed to address at the individual student level.

> *"The mismatch between education and skills and job requirements remains a key development challenge for African countries."* — Morsy & Mukasa (2020, p. 1)

---

**Njifen, I. (2024). Sub-Saharan Africa's higher education: investment decisions on human capital in the presence of youth unemployment.**  
*Studies in Higher Education*, 49(2), 351–367.  
DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1080/03075079.2023.2234397](https://doi.org/10.1080/03075079.2023.2234397)

Using panel data from 40 Sub-Saharan African countries covering the period 2000–2020, this study demonstrates that youth unemployment has an **inverse relationship with higher education enrollment** in countries with low human development. The research highlights that increased university enrollment without corresponding skills relevance does not reduce unemployment — reinforcing the need for platforms that make skills visible and actionable during the education phase.

---

**ILO. (2024). Global Employment Trends for Youth 2024: Sub-Saharan Africa Regional Brief.**  
International Labour Organization. Geneva.  
URL: [https://www.ilo.org/publications/employment-trends-youth-sub-saharan-africa](https://www.ilo.org/publications/employment-trends-youth-sub-saharan-africa)  
PDF: [https://www.ilo.org/sites/default/files/2024-08/Sub-Saharan%20Africa%20GET%20Youth%202024_0.pdf](https://www.ilo.org/sites/default/files/2024-08/Sub-Saharan%20Africa%20GET%20Youth%202024_0.pdf)

The ILO's authoritative 2024 report establishes the scale of the challenge: **3 in 4 African youth lack secure, stable employment**, and Sub-Saharan Africa's youth labour force is projected to grow by 76 million persons by 2050. The report flags the urgency of skill-relevant education reform and digital tools that connect youth competencies to labour market demands. SkillsSync directly targets this intersection.

---

**Njifen, I. (2024). Education-Job mismatch, earnings and worker's satisfaction in African labor market: evidence from Cameroon.**  
*Economia Politica*, 41, 931–962.  
DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1007/s40888-024-00345-y](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40888-024-00345-y)

This econometric study quantifies the **wage penalties and job dissatisfaction** associated with education-job mismatch in an African labor market. It finds that mismatched workers earn significantly less and report lower job satisfaction — establishing the personal economic cost of the skills gap that motivates SkillsSync's solution.

## 2. NLP and Large Language Models for Skill Extraction

SkillsSync's core technical innovation is the application of NLP and transformer-based LLMs to extract competency signals from unstructured academic documents. The following papers establish the state of the art in this field and directly inform SkillsSync's NLP pipeline architecture.

---

**Zhang, M., Jensen, K. N., Sonniks, S. D., & Plank, B. (2022). SkillSpan: Hard and Soft Skill Extraction from English Job Postings.**  
In *Proceedings of the 2022 Conference of the North American Chapter of the Association for Computational Linguistics: Human Language Technologies*, pp. 4962–4984. Seattle, USA.  
DOI: [https://doi.org/10.18653/v1/2022.naacl-main.366](https://doi.org/10.18653/v1/2022.naacl-main.366)  
arXiv: [https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.12811](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.12811)  
ACL Anthology: [https://aclanthology.org/2022.naacl-main.366/](https://aclanthology.org/2022.naacl-main.366/)

The most-cited benchmark paper in NLP-based skill extraction. SkillSpan introduces a **14,500-sentence, 12,500+ span annotated dataset** for hard and soft skill extraction, along with fine-tuned BERT and domain-adapted JobBERT models. The authors demonstrate that domain-adapted transformer models significantly outperform general-purpose language models on skill span detection. SkillsSync's NLP architecture for extracting competency spans from educational documents directly mirrors this approach, adapted for academic rather than job-posting text.

> *"We introduce SKILLSPAN, a novel and challenging skill extraction dataset consisting of 14.5K sentences..."* — Zhang et al. (2022, p. 4962)

---

**Zhang, M., van der Goot, R., & Plank, B. (2023). ESCOXLM-R: Multilingual Taxonomy-driven Pre-training for the Job Market Domain.**  
In *Proceedings of the 61st Annual Meeting of the Association for Computational Linguistics* (Volume 1: Long Papers), pp. 11871–11890. Toronto, Canada.  
DOI: [https://doi.org/10.18653/v1/2023.acl-long.662](https://doi.org/10.18653/v1/2023.acl-long.662)  
arXiv: [https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.12092](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.12092)  
ACL Anthology: [https://aclanthology.org/2023.acl-long.662/](https://aclanthology.org/2023.acl-long.662/)

Introduces **ESCOXLM-R**, a multilingual model pre-trained on the European Skills, Competences, Qualifications and Occupations (ESCO) taxonomy across 27 languages using dynamic masked language modeling and taxonomic relation objectives. This paper both documents the ESCO taxonomy's structure (13,890 skills, competences, and knowledge items; 2,942 occupations) and provides the foundational multilingual model that SkillsSync's competency mapping engine is benchmarked against. The multilingual capability is particularly important for deployment across diverse African linguistic contexts.

---

**Senger, E., Zhang, M., van der Goot, R., & Plank, B. (2024). Deep Learning-based Computational Job Market Analysis: A Survey on Skill Extraction and Classification from Job Postings.**  
In *Proceedings of the First Workshop on NLP for Human Resources (NLP4HR 2024)*, EACL. Malta.  
DOI: [https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2402.05617](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2402.05617)  
arXiv: [https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.05617](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.05617)  
ACL Anthology: [https://aclanthology.org/2024.nlp4hr-1.1/](https://aclanthology.org/2024.nlp4hr-1.1/)

A comprehensive survey of deep learning methods for skill extraction and classification, cataloguing available datasets, documenting terminology inconsistencies across the field (hard/soft skills, technical/non-technical), and systematically comparing state-of-the-art approaches. This is the primary methodological reference for SkillsSync's NLP architecture design decisions.

---

**Herandi, A., Li, Y., Liu, Z., Hu, X., & Cai, X. (2024). Skill-LLM: Repurposing General-Purpose LLMs for Skill Extraction.**  
arXiv:2410.12052 [cs.CL]. October 15, 2024.  
DOI: [https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2410.12052](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2410.12052)  
arXiv: [https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.12052](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.12052)

Demonstrates that **fine-tuned LLMs outperform zero-shot and few-shot GPT-4** for skill extraction from job descriptions, establishing the efficiency-accuracy trade-off critical to SkillsSync's production deployment. The paper's methodology — repurposing and fine-tuning general LLMs (such as Google Gemini, which SkillsSync uses) specifically for skill extraction — directly validates SkillsSync's approach.

---

**Zhang, M., Jensen, K. N., van der Goot, R., & Plank, B. (2022). Skill Extraction from Job Postings using Weak Supervision.**  
In *Proceedings of the Workshop on Recommender Systems for Human Resources (RecSys in HR'22)*, in conjunction with ACM RecSys 2022. Seattle, USA.  
arXiv: [https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.08071](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.08071)  
Proceedings PDF: [https://ceur-ws.org/Vol-3218/RecSysHR2022-paper_10.pdf](https://ceur-ws.org/Vol-3218/RecSysHR2022-paper_10.pdf)

Proposes a **weak supervision approach** leveraging ESCO taxonomy embeddings to extract skills without expensive manual annotation. This methodology is critical for SkillsSync's scalability: manual annotation of educational documents across 1,500+ students is economically unfeasible, and weak supervision using existing skill taxonomies provides a scalable alternative.

---

**Bhola, A., Halder, K., Prasad, A., & Kan, M.-Y. (2020). Retrieving Skills from Job Descriptions: A Language Model Based Extreme Multi-label Classification Framework.**  
In *Proceedings of the 28th International Conference on Computational Linguistics (COLING 2020)*, pp. 5832–5842. Barcelona (Online).  
ACL Anthology: [https://aclanthology.org/2020.coling-main.513/](https://aclanthology.org/2020.coling-main.513/)

Frames skill retrieval as an **extreme multi-label classification (XMLC) problem** over thousands of skill labels, achieving 9%+ improvement over baseline in recall and normalized discounted cumulative gain (nDCG). SkillsSync's skill tagging system, which must classify extracted competency signals into hundreds of distinct skill categories, is directly analogous to this XMLC framework.

---

**Otani, N., Bhutani, N., & Hruschka, E. (2024). Natural Language Processing for Human Resources: A Survey.**  
arXiv:2410.16498 [cs.CL]. October 21, 2024.  
DOI: [https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2410.16498](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2410.16498)  
arXiv: [https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.16498](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.16498)

A comprehensive NLP-for-HR survey covering information extraction, text classification, recommendation systems, and language generation, along with ethical considerations for AI in human capital contexts. Provides the broader scientific context for SkillsSync's use of NLP in the talent and education domain.

---

**Gaur, B., Saluja, G. S., Sivakumar, H. B., & Singh, S. (2021). Semi-supervised deep learning based named entity recognition model to parse education section of resumes.**  
*Neural Computing and Applications*, 33(11), 5705–5718.  
DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1007/s00521-020-05351-2](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00521-020-05351-2)  
Springer: [https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00521-020-05351-2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00521-020-05351-2)

A semi-supervised Named Entity Recognition (NER) model trained to extract **educational qualifications, institutions, and degrees** from document education sections — directly analogous to SkillsSync's parsing of academic transcripts and certificates. The semi-supervised approach is particularly relevant for educational contexts where labeled training data is scarce.

---

**Kesim, E., & Deliahmetoglu, A. (2023). Named entity recognition in resumes.**  
arXiv:2306.13062 [cs.CL]. June 22, 2023.  
arXiv: [https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.13062](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.13062)

Benchmarks six transformer-based NER models on resume data, recognizing eight entity types including **skills, job titles, degrees, and programming languages**. Provides an architectural comparison that directly informs SkillsSync's entity extraction module design.

## 4. Optical Character Recognition for Handwritten Academic Documents

SkillsSync accepts handwritten academic documents (notes, assignments, handwritten coursework) as input, converting them to machine-readable text before NLP processing. The following research establishes the state of the art in Handwritten Text Recognition (HTR).

---

**AlKendi, W., Gechter, F., Heyberger, L., & Guyeux, C. (2024). Advancements and Challenges in Handwritten Text Recognition: A Comprehensive Survey.**  
*Journal of Imaging*, 10(1), 18. MDPI.  
DOI: [https://doi.org/10.3390/jimaging10010018](https://doi.org/10.3390/jimaging10010018)  
Open Access: [https://www.mdpi.com/2313-433X/10/1/18](https://www.mdpi.com/2313-433X/10/1/18)  
PubMed: [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38249003/](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38249003/)

A comprehensive 2024 survey of HTR systems, covering the evolution from heuristic rule-based methods to state-of-the-art deep neural models. The survey systematically reviews **CNN-LSTM-CTC architectures, attention-based encoder-decoder models, and transformer-based HTR systems**, evaluating them across datasets including IAM, RIMES, and BENTHAM. This is the primary technical reference for SkillsSync's handwritten document OCR pipeline design.

---

**Li, M., Lv, T., Cui, L., Lu, Y., Florencio, D., Zhang, C., Li, Z., & Wei, F. (2021). TrOCR: Transformer-based Optical Character Recognition with Pre-trained Models.**  
Microsoft Research. arXiv:2109.10282 [cs.CL]. September 2021.  
DOI: [https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2109.10282](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2109.10282)  
arXiv: [https://arxiv.org/abs/2109.10282](https://arxiv.org/abs/2109.10282)  
Microsoft Research: [https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/publication/trocr-transformer-based-optical-character-recognition-with-pre-trained-models/](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/publication/trocr-transformer-based-optical-character-recognition-with-pre-trained-models/)

Introduces **TrOCR**, an end-to-end transformer encoder-decoder model for handwritten and printed text recognition that achieves state-of-the-art results on standard HTR benchmarks (IAM, SROIE). The model uses pre-trained image transformers (BEiT) and text transformers (RoBERTa/GPT-2) in an encoder-decoder architecture, eliminating the need for CNN feature extractors. SkillsSync's handwritten assignment processing pipeline is architected based on TrOCR and its successors.

## 5. Job Market Competency Frameworks and Skill Taxonomies

SkillsSync maps extracted student competencies against standardized skill taxonomies aligned with real-time job market requirements. The following works establish the scientific basis for competency frameworks and dynamic skill-to-job matching.

---

**Zhang, M., van der Goot, R., & Plank, B. (2023). ESCOXLM-R: Multilingual Taxonomy-driven Pre-training for the Job Market Domain.**  
*(See full citation in Section 2)*  
DOI: [https://doi.org/10.18653/v1/2023.acl-long.662](https://doi.org/10.18653/v1/2023.acl-long.662)

Also documents the structure of the **ESCO taxonomy** — the European Skills, Competences, Qualifications and Occupations framework — which contains 13,890 skills, competences, and knowledge items, and 2,942 occupations across 27 languages. ESCO provides the structured competency ontology that SkillsSync's extracted skills are mapped against, enabling standardized comparison between student profiles and employer requirements.

---

**de Groot, M., Schutte, J., & Graus, D. (2021). Job Posting-Enriched Knowledge Graph for Skills-based Matching.**  
Presented at *Recommender Systems for Human Resources Workshop (RecSys in HR 2021)*. Amsterdam, Netherlands.  
arXiv: [https://arxiv.org/abs/2109.02554](https://arxiv.org/abs/2109.02554)

Builds a **Skills & Occupation Knowledge Graph** by enriching ESCO and O*NET taxonomies with live job posting data, then uses link prediction and shortest-path algorithms for career pathway generation. This paper directly informs SkillsSync's dynamic skill-to-job mapping architecture — including the career roadmap generation that identifies the shortest learning path from a student's current skill profile to a target occupation's requirements.

---

**Morsy, H., & Mukasa, A. (2020). 'Mind the mismatch?' Incidence, drivers, and persistence of African youths' skill and educational mismatches.**

## 6. AI-Powered Career Development and Roadmapping Systems

The career guidance and personalized roadmapping features of SkillsSync are grounded in an emerging body of research on AI-driven career development systems.

---

**Gedrimiene, E., Celik, I., Kaasila, A., Mikkonen, K., & Muukkonen, H. (2024). Artificial Intelligence (AI)-enhanced learning analytics (LA) for supporting career decisions: advantages and challenges from user perspective.**  
*(See full citation in Section 3)*  
DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1007/s10639-023-12277-4](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10639-023-12277-4)

Specifically identifies **personalization, data integration, and real-time feedback** as the key differentiating characteristics of effective AI career guidance systems versus static career counseling. These are the exact architectural features SkillsSync implements: personalized profiles, integration of academic and market data, and dynamic readiness scoring.

The survey includes a dedicated section on **skill-to-job matching algorithms and career recommendation** as downstream applications of skill classification, covering techniques from rule-based matching to graph neural networks. This section directly informs SkillsSync's career pathway recommendation engine.

*(See full citation in Section 5)*

The **career pathfinding algorithms** in this paper — specifically, shortest-path traversal over a skills knowledge graph — provide the mathematical framework for SkillsSync's learning roadmap generation. The approach identifies the minimum number of skill acquisitions required to bridge the gap between a student's current profile and a target job's requirements.

DORIS's DeepFM architecture for **personalized course sequencing** — selecting the right learning resources in the right order based on a learner's current skills and learning objectives — is directly analogous to SkillsSync's roadmap generation module.
